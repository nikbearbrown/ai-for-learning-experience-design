## ⚠️ Critical — Requires Author Attention

**ONE CONTRADICTED finding (dimension-level mischaracterization of Steiss et al. 2024).** The chapter's running claim that expert human feedback "wins on the dimensions that matter most for learning — notably localization … and substantive criteria-linked commentary," with "AI feedback … competitive on clarity and tone," **inverts the paper's actual result.** Steiss et al. found human raters better "in all categories *other than* criteria-based" — i.e., humans won on *clear directions, accuracy, essential features, and supportive tone*; the single dimension where AI was **not** beaten was **criteria-based**. So:
- "AI competitive on clarity and tone" — contradicted; humans won on supportive tone **and** clear directions.
- "humans win on … criteria-linked commentary" — contradicted; criteria-based is the one dimension AI matched.
- "localization" is **not a scored dimension** in the study (the five were criteria-based, clear directions, accuracy, essential features, supportive tone). It is defensible only as a *qualitative* observation (humans made in-line marks at specific spots; ChatGPT wrote a single global note).

The chapter's **directional headline is correct** (well-trained human evaluators beat ChatGPT overall: 4.0 vs 3.6 on a 5-pt scale; largest human advantage on "essential features" = developmental appropriateness). But the specific dimension attributions are wrong and should be rewritten. Note also: per the lead author (Graham) and the study, ChatGPT was *slightly better* on feedback about reasoning/argumentation/use of evidence — a nuance worth not erasing. Flagged inline.

No OUTDATED claims. No other CONTRADICTED claims.

### EVIDENCE — **CONTRADICTED** (dimension-level; see Critical)
**Sentence:** "expert human feedback wins on the dimensions that matter most for learning — notably localization … and substantive criteria-linked commentary (Steiss et al. 2024, *Learning and Instruction*). AI feedback was competitive on clarity and tone, weaker on the judgment-heavy dimensions …"
**Finding:** Authors/venue CONFIRMED — Steiss, J., Tate, T., Graham, S., Cruz, J., Hebert, M., Wang, J., Moon, Y., Tseng, W., Warschauer, M., & Olson, C. B. (2024), *Learning and Instruction* 91, 101894, DOI 10.1016/j.learninstruc.2024.101894. **Result CONTRADICTED at the dimension level** (verified via abstract + full detailed account, Hechinger/Barshay 2024). "Localization" is not a scored construct; "clarity and tone" were human wins, not AI parity. **Rewrite recommended.** Flagged inline.
**Reference:** Steiss et al. (2024), *Learning and Instruction* 91, 101894. DOI 10.1016/j.learninstruc.2024.101894

### EVIDENCE — DIRECTION SUPPORTED (resolves [verify], no single anchor)
**Sentence:** "Gains from teacher feedback show better retention than gains from AI feedback, and learners receiving AI feedback tend to make fewer and shallower revisions … [verify — these findings are from research notes]."
**Finding:** No single paper anchors both clauses, but **both directions are supported in the comparative-feedback literature.** (a) Retention: an Open Praxis study on informative-text writing found *teacher* feedback produced a significant retention-test gain (large effect) while AI feedback did not (small effect). (b) Revision depth: "Generative AI offers more, but students revise less" (*IJETHE* 2026) and related work report higher-quality AI feedback not translating into greater revision, with engagement/uptake — not feedback quality — driving transfer. Recommend the chapter cite these as *illustrative* rather than implying a single settled result. The heavy `[verify]` can be downgraded to "direction supported; specific anchors illustrative." Treated as **resolved-to-direction.**
**Reference (illustrative):** "The Effect of Feedback on Informative Text Writing: AI or Teacher?" *Open Praxis* 17(3); "Generative AI offers more, but students revise less," *IJETHE* (2026), DOI 10.1186/s41239-026-00579-9.

### SPECIALIST — CONFIRMED (correctly framed as pre-LLM/diagnostic)
**Sentence:** "MIT's Les Perelman and colleagues built BABEL … The gibberish reliably earned top scores."
**Finding:** CONFIRMED. Perelman's BABEL ("Basic Automatic B.S. The chapter's framing — "predates LLMs … scores the *measurable proxies* of the construct, not the construct" — is the project's exact lesson. Correctly labeled diagnostic, not a current-product claim.
**Reference:** Perelman, L. — BABEL Generator / "The BABEL Generator and E-Rater" (lesperelman.com). Coverage: *Chronicle of Higher Education* (2014).
