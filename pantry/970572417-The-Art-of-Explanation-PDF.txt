 Understanding Explanation

 For many, the term "explanation" is taken for granted. We
 explain things without considering what makes an
 explanation truly effective. Like a dancer who understands
the unique details of different dance forms, it is essential to
define what an explanation is to improve our skills.

What Is Not An Explanation

Understanding what constitutes an explanation can be aided
by recognizing alternative communication forms. Here are
some examples that are not explanations:

Description

A description directly depicts an idea or object with the
intent to help visualize it.

Definition

A definition provides precise meanings to clarify something.

Instruction

Instructions guide a person on how to perform an action or
achieve a result.

Elaboration
Elaboration offers detailed presentations on concepts to
enhance understanding.

Report

A report recounts facts and details about an event.

Illustration

An illustration serves to provide examples that clarify an
idea.
While these forms have value, they differ significantly from
explanation, which aims to increase understanding.

Defining Explanation

An explanation is a process that makes facts understandable.
According to Merriam-Webster, it’s about clarifying and
making things known. In essence, an explanation describes
facts in a way that fosters understanding, distinguishing it
from other informational forms due to its intent.

Explanations Require Empathy
Effective explainers possess empathy, enabling them to see
things from their audience's perspective. This ability allows
them to craft explanations that resonate with the audience’s
experiences, thereby enhancing comprehension.

Act and Art

Creating explanations is both a science (fact-making) and an
art (communicating ideas uniquely). Facts alone can be
overwhelming without context, and effective explanations
can make them accessible and relatable.

Look at Your Fish

Developing a skill for explanation is about perspective. It
involves taking familiar ideas and transforming them into
packaged explanations that reveal deeper insights.

Explanation Lowers the Cost of Understanding

In a world overflowing with information, explanations help
minimize the effort needed to comprehend complex topics.
By presenting ideas clearly, they encourage engagement with
unfamiliar subjects.

An Explanation Is a Way to Package Ideas

The art of explanation involves transforming facts into
understandable formats. It requires considering context and
themes to provide clarity on certain topics.

Explanations Answer the Question “Why?”

Explanations indicate not only what things are but also why
they matter. This relevance makes them essential for
grasping the significance of information.

Explanations Make People Care

An effective explanation sparks interest and motivation in the
audience. By presenting facts in a way that evokes curiosity,
it helps people see the relevance of new ideas.
In summary, understanding how to effectively explain
concepts enhances not only the audience's comprehension but
also the explainer's insight into the subject matter. Engaging
with the process of explanation is a valuable skill that
benefits everyone involved.

Storytelling in Explanation



The Role of Story in Communication

- Stories are essential for effective explanations.
- They can make ideas more relatable, offering an alternative
to dry facts commonly seen in professional communications.
- Examples show how the same information can be presented
through storytelling versus fact telling.

Facts Versus Stories

- Historically, storytelling and fact telling were distinct; facts
dominated professional contexts while stories were reserved
for personal contexts.
- Combining both is necessary: facts provide substance while
stories offer meaning, enhancing the impact of explanations.

The Essence of Explanation

- Effective explanations incorporate human experiences,
allowing audiences to relate to and retain information.
- The storytelling technique should not follow a traditional
narrative arc but should instead bring the audience into a
relatable experience.

Application in Common Craft

- Common Craft’s popularity stems from embedding stories
within technical explanations, showcasing real-world
applications of technology.
- A specific example includes a video on wikis that tells the
story of friends planning a camping trip using a wiki, making
the technology relatable.

The Simple Ingredient: People
- Stories serve as a human wrapper for ideas, enhancing their
appeal and relatability.
- By using simple narratives featuring characters with needs,
explanations become more engaging.

Using Stories in Explanations

- Writing effective scripts starts by focusing on core facts,
followed by incorporating story elements that highlight
human experiences and needs.
- More focus on the experiences of individuals can make
complex ideas easier to understand.

Basic Story Format

- A simple storytelling structure can enhance explanations:
  1. Introduce a relatable character.
  2. Present a problem that character faces.
  3. Develop a solution that resolves the issue.
  4. Relate this to the audience’s desires or needs.

Limitations of Storytelling

- Storytelling isn’t universally applicable; it depends on
context:
 - Process-oriented explanations might require
straightforward instruction rather than narrative.
 - Organizational culture, time constraints, and the inherent

storytelling.

Personification in Explanations

- When human experiences are unavailable, personification
can be a tool to create engaging narratives around abstract
concepts.
- For instance, relating the behavior of electrons to familiar
experiences makes complex scientific ideas more accessible.

Summary

- To effectively explain concepts, it’s crucial to weave facts
into relatable stories that incorporate human elements,
allowing audiences to connect with and understand the
content more deeply.

Introduction to Movie Pitches

Hollywood directors and screenwriters face significant
challenges when pitching their movie ideas to studios, which

The Power of Connection

A notable example involved Dan O’Bannon and Ron Shusett,
who pitched their alien movie idea as “Jaws in Space.” By
referencing a well-known film, they helped studio executives

Old Versus New Ideas
Many new products are simply innovative solutions to old
problems. To explain new concepts effectively, one can relate
them to something widely understood (e.g., e-mail as “letters
via computers”). Adding context about the pains of the old
method enhances understanding by demonstrating how the
new solution improves on it.

Building on Existing Knowledge

Successful explanations build on existing knowledge rather
than trying to establish entirely new ideas. The comparisons
simplify complex concepts, making them easier for
audiences to grasp. This approach is crucial for both product
marketing and general explanations.

Options for Effective Explanation

When explaining a new concept, one can either establish new
ideas (which can overwhelm) or connect them to familiar
experiences (which promotes understanding). For instance,
when explaining clouds to a child, referencing boiling water
is more effective than delving into scientific details.
The Role of Analogy

Analogies play a key role in explanations by highlighting
connections between ideas. They clarify concepts through
relatable comparisons rather than merely serving as figures of
speech. Examples include comparing *Alien* to *Jaws*,
e-mail to traditional mail, and clouds to steam.

Application in Common Craft Videos

In creating educational content, Common Craft often uses
analogies to connect difficult concepts to familiar
frameworks. For example, explaining augmented reality
through the perspective of fighter pilots helps audiences
understand its function without prior knowledge.

Conclusion

Connections are vital to making complex ideas
comprehensible. By relating unfamiliar information to known
concepts, educators and marketers can foster confidence and
encourage deeper exploration. The underlying goal is to
engage the audience by saying, “You know X, right? Well, Y
is similar, and here’s how.” The following chapter will delve
into the element of description in explanations.

Chapter 10 Summary : Simplification

Understanding the Curse of Knowledge and
Simplification

We all experience the curse of knowledge, which often leads
to overly complex explanations. This is particularly evident
in technical subjects, like virtualization in computer software,
where the expert's perspective can create barriers for novices.



The Challenge of Complexity

Experts often fail to predict what is simple for others,
resulting in explanations that are:
- Accurate but incomprehensible
- Too detailed
- Filled with jargon
- Lacking context

Simplification Guidelines

To improve understanding, experts should simplify complex
ideas by:
- Not making assumptions about the audience's prior
knowledge
- Using basic language
- Looking at the subject from a broad perspective
- Focusing on overarching concepts rather than details

Case Study: Steve and Martha

Steve, a software salesperson, struggled to explain
virtualization to his aunt Martha. Initially, he felt
overwhelmed by the complexity and avoided the discussion.
Later, he realized he could simplify the explanation using the
following strategies:
1.
Identify Common Knowledge
: Recognize what Martha already understands about
computers.
2.
Use Simple Language
: Replace technical terms with accessible language.
3.
Zoom Out
: Consider the high-level reasons virtualization exists, such as
maximizing computer resources.
4.
Focus on Big Ideas
: Break down the concept into graspable components,
avoiding detail-heavy jargon.
5.
Prioritize Understanding Over Accuracy
: Relate virtualization to familiar concepts, like her home
computer.
Steve explained virtualization by comparing it to maximizing
the power of a personal computer, making it comprehensible
for Martha.

Key Takeaways for Effective Explanation

To effectively explain complex ideas:
- Assess your audience's understanding
- Avoid jargon and overly technical language
- Emphasize main ideas over trivial details
- Establish connections to familiar concepts
- Simplify down to the core idea, reducing complexity and
enhancing understanding
In conclusion, simplification is key to effective
communication, especially in technical subjects. By focusing
on understanding rather than detail, experts can bridge the
gap and facilitate greater comprehension.
Critical Thinking
Key Point:The importance of audience awareness in
effective communication.
Critical Interpretation:The chapter emphasizes that
experts often fall victim to the 'curse of knowledge,'
which makes it difficult for them to simplify and tailor
their explanations to the audience's level of
understanding. This suggests a limitation in the author's
viewpoint—that while simplification is important, the

Furthermore, if the audience's prior knowledge is not

Chapter 11 Summary : Constraints

Understanding Constraints in Explanations

Constraints play a crucial role in transforming ideas into
clear and comprehensible explanations. While the abundance
of options can evoke feelings of overwhelm and frustration,
implementing constraints allows for more focused
decision-making.

The Paradox of Choice

Psychologist Barry Schwartz discusses the negative effects of
too many choices in his book *Paradox of Choice*. Research
shows that overwhelming options often lead to
dissatisfaction and decision paralysis. For instance, an
experiment with jam varieties revealed that consumers were
less likely to purchase a jar when faced with a larger
selection compared to a smaller one.

The Role of Constraints

In crafting explanations, constraints help narrow down
overwhelming options, fostering confidence in the
decision-making process. For example, deciding to only
consider dark green ties simplifies the selection, highlighting
relevant factors that may have been overlooked.

Applying Constraints: The Common Craft
Approach

The Common Craft team employs specific constraints in
