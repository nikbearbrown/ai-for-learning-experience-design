About the book
In *The Creative Habit*, Twyla Tharp demystifies creativity,
asserting that it is not a divine gift but a skill accessible to
anyone willing to cultivate it. Drawing from her extensive
thirty-five-year career, Tharp offers practical guidance and
insightful exercises to help readers embrace creativity as a
habitual practice integral to everyday life. Whether you're an
artist, entrepreneur, or parent, her exercises—ranging from
observational prompts to mental games—are designed to
kickstart your imagination and lead you through creative
blocks. Tharp emphasizes the importance of rituals,
self-awareness, and organization in nurturing sustained
creativity, transforming moments of uncertainty into
opportunities for inspiration. With an optimistic approach, she
invites you to view the empty canvas not as a source of
anxiety, but as a space full of potential, encouraging you to
take that vital first step in your creative journey.

Summary of Chapter 1: The Creative Habit



Setting the Scene

Twyla Tharp describes standing in a large, empty dance
studio in Manhattan, about to embark on creating a new
dance program. She has half of the program ready but the rest
remains unknown, emphasizing the weight of expectations
from dancers and presenters who rely on her creativity.

The Pressure of Creation

Tharp reflects on the pressure that comes with starting from a
blank space, drawing parallels with other creative disciplines
like writing, painting, and composing music. The empty
room symbolizes both the challenge and the fear of
confronting creativity, a moment that paralyzes many.

Experience and Identity

As a seasoned choreographer, Tharp acknowledges her
extensive experience creating over 130 dances and ballets,
which allows her to find peace and promise in the daunting




                                                      Scan to Download
task of creation. She views creativity as a critical part of her
identity and a continual challenge she embraces.

Routine and Discipline

Tharp highlights the importance of structured routines in
enhancing creativity. Like accomplished writers and artists,
she emphasizes that creativity thrives on disciplined habits,
making the creative process more efficient and productive.

Creativity Beyond the Arts

She asserts that creativity is not exclusive to the arts but is
essential in various fields, including business and parenting.
Through her experiences, Tharp intends to teach others about
fostering creativity, applying practical exercises to challenge
and develop one’s creative skills.

The Truth about Genius

Disputing the notion of "natural" geniuses, Tharp emphasizes
the role of hard work and preparation in achieving creativity.
Using the example of Mozart, she illustrates that talent
requires diligent practice, discipline, and skill development.

Preparation as Key to Creativity

Tharp discusses the paradox of creativity as both a fresh
concept and a process grounded in routine. Skill building
through repetition and training is essential for turning
imaginative ideas into reality.

Lessons from The Karate Kid

She recalls a scene from "The Karate Kid" to illustrate the
subtlety of learning through practice, showing that
foundational skills learned in preparation can lead to
remarkable results when creativity is needed.

Conclusion

Tharp concludes by asserting her belief that anyone can
become more creative through intentional preparation and
practice, arguing that creativity should be a habit rather than
a sporadic inspiration. Reading her book and implementing
its suggestions will help eliminate the fear associated with
starting anew.

Critical Thinking
Key Point:The Power of Routine in Creativity
Critical Interpretation:Tharp’s assertion that routine
fosters creativity invites debate on whether creativity
can truly flourish from structured habits or if it stems
from spontaneous, unrestrained inspiration. Critics may
argue that strict routines could stifle individual
expression, suggesting creativity could thrive better in
environments that embrace chaos and unpredictability.
For example, renowned writer Anne Lamott emphasizes
the importance of free-flowing ideas over rigid
discipline in her works, highlighting that creativity often
emerges from a space of freedom rather than constraint.
Therefore, while Tharp’s viewpoint is compelling, it
may overlook the potential benefits of a more fluid, less
structured approach to creative endeavors.

Summary of Chapter 7: Creativity and Planning



The Importance of Planning

- Productive artists have clear goals and plans to guide their
work but must avoid the pitfalls of overplanning.
- Richard Avedon exemplifies this balance; while he prepares
meticulously for his photography, he remains flexible and
open to spontaneity once the shoot begins.

The Creative Paradox

- Successful creativity requires both thorough preparation
and a willingness to let go of rigid plans to embrace
unexpected moments of inspiration.
- Luck plays a crucial role in creativity but is often a
byproduct of being adequately prepared and open to
accidental discoveries.

Mistakes in Planning

- Common planning errors include:
  - Relying too much on others, leading to disappointment
when teamwork falters.
  - Perfectionism, which can stifle progress by preventing

  - Locking into the wrong structure, failing to adapt to
changes in project requirements or dynamics.
  - Feeling obligated to pursue projects that do not resonate,
rather than following inspiration and genuine interest.
  - Working with incorrect materials, such as attempting a
project with fewer resources than needed.

Generosity and Collaboration

Mistake of Over-Explaining

Twyla Tharp reflects on her experience of announcing a
ballet based on Euripides' *The Bacchae*, realizing that
over-explaining her artistic intentions can distract the
audience, as they might strive to find the connections that
guide her creativity.

Defining the Spine

The "spine" is defined as the initial strong idea or core
intention behind a creative work. It serves as an internal
compass for the artist, outlining themes and structures that
may not be explicitly communicated to the audience.

Journey from Idea to Creation




                                                       Scan to Download
Tharp illustrates the evolution of her ballet *Surfer at the
River Styx*, which drew inspiration from *The Bacchae*.
Although the original story was rich, she distilled the essence
down to the theme of hubris, transforming it into a narrative
arc that ultimately defined the dance.

The Necessity of Spine for Art

Every work of art needs a spine—an underlying theme that
guides its creation. This spine can remain hidden from the
audience but is essential for the artist in maintaining focus
and coherence throughout the creative process.

Inspiration from Literature

Tharp discusses Bernard Malamud's *The Natural*, which
drew inspiration from real-life events but was fundamentally
structured around a more profound spine—the quest for the
Holy Grail. This parallel highlights the distinction between a
story's surface and its deeper thematic elements.

Tharp reflects on her own evolution in utilizing the spine

 External critique can help clarify intentions.
-

 Simplifying complex ideas by explaining them in basic

Tharp shares personal processes for defining the spine in

The Role of Music as Spine

Efficiency and Productivity

A well-defined spine encourages clarity and efficiency in the
creative process, allowing artists to remain focused and
productive without losing track in the midst of creativity.

Final Thoughts

Ultimately, the spine is a personal tool that artists develop to
aid their creative practice. Having a spine allows for greater
exploration and innovation, while maintaining a clear
direction and purpose in their work. Tharp emphasizes the

Summary of Chapter 10: Getting Out of Ruts and

Introduction to Ruts

Creativity can sometimes fail us despite our best efforts,
leading to moments where we feel stuck or in a rut.
Differentiating between being in a rut and experiencing
writer's block is crucial. A rut implies you're still moving
forward but making no real progress, while writer's block
indicates a complete halt.

Identifying Ruts

Ruts can arise from various factors, including bad ideas, bad
timing, bad luck, or sticking to outdated methods. Signs of
being in a rut include stagnation, frustration, and a sense of
déjà vu. It's important to monitor your work and recognize
when you're in a rut.

2.

3.

challenging but rewarding.

Environmental Influences

Sometimes external factors can induce negativity towards
your work. Changing your environment or routine can
provide new perspectives and energy.

Generating New Ideas

To escape a rut, consider aggressive quotas for ideas,

Challenging Assumptions

Finding Your Groove

A groove is a state where creativity flows effortlessly,
marked by excitement and clarity. Achieving it often requires
navigating through ruts initially. Grooves can vary in
duration—from short bursts to extended periods of creative
productivity.

Using Rituals and Exercises

Practices such as ‘Do a Verb’ and building a bridge to the

Types of Failure

Failure of Skill

5.
Failure through Repetition

6.
Failure of Denial
: Ignoring issues leads to greater problems. Acknowledging
and addressing failures can turn them into opportunities for
improvement.
