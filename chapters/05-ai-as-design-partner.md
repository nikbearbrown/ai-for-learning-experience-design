## Opening case: Two microlessons, one novice, and the year-three question

Two microlessons on the same topic, built by the same novice designer in the same graduate course, two weeks apart. One was built with GPT-4 at her elbow; one unassisted. Side by side, the difference is not subtle. The AI-assisted lesson has cleaner objectives, tighter activity-assessment alignment, fewer rookie moves — the wall-of-text screen, the quiz question testing trivia the lesson never taught. Blind raters using a five-criteria rubric scored it higher.

This is not an anecdote. It is the actual design of a field experiment inside a 14-week graduate educational technology course: 27 students, eight microlessons each across four modules, alternating between AI-assisted and unassisted work, rubric-scored. The result: AI-assisted microlessons scored significantly higher for half the assignments and **never scored lower on average** (Moore et al. 2025). Within the same designer — controlling for talent, motivation, the lot — the assisted artifact was sometimes better and never worse.

So the A/B test has a winner: if the question is "should novices use AI to draft microlessons," the evidence says yes. Sit with that. This book's spine is a study where the AI condition made learners *worse*; here is a careful study where it made novice designers' *work* better. Both are true. Neither contradicts the other.

Because now ask the question the A/B test cannot answer. The dependent variable was the *lesson*, not the *designer*. Every data point is compatible with two futures: one where assisted practice accelerates the novice toward expert judgment, and one where the assignments the AI helped complete were precisely the practice problems through which design judgment used to get built — and the novice arrives at year three fluent in prompts and poor in pedagogy. Better drafts today and atrophied judgment in three years are fully compatible with all the evidence the field has.

You spent Chapter 2 learning that learners under load rationally take the shortcut, and Chapter 4 learning what to do when the most important column in the evidence table is empty. This chapter is where you discover that the learner in question is you. Which workflow produces the better designer in year three? Nobody knows. You still have to decide on Monday. That is what the chapter is for.

## Core content

### What designers actually do with GenAI: the practitioner evidence

Three peer-reviewed practitioner studies form the empirical base, and they agree on the shape of adoption. Luo, Muljana, Ren and Young (2025, *ETR&D* 73(2), 741–766 — mixed methods, verified): designers use generative AI most for ideation, low-stakes drafts (learning objectives, activity ideas — "overcoming designer's block"), streamlining, and collaboration support; their documented concerns are quality, privacy (sensitive data leaking into prompts), ownership and authorship, plagiarism, and absent organizational guidelines. Yang and Stefaniak (2025, *ETR&D* [verify — synthesis-sourced; bibliographic record unpinned]): ChatGPT for content generation, writing improvement, problem-solving, communication, and information searching — with attitudes splitting into optimistic, wary, and pessimistic camps rather than one adoption story. Kumar et al. (2024, *Online Learning* [verify — synthesis-sourced]) add the finding with the largest career implication: higher-ed instructional designers use GenAI not only for design work but as **institutional translators** — guiding faculty use, building training, shaping policy. The LX designer is becoming the organization's de facto AI-governance layer — precisely the role this book trains.

The pattern across all three: the established gains are *upstream* — ideation, drafting, formatting, summarizing — and the unresolved questions are *downstream* — quality assurance, pedagogical judgment, authorship, expertise development. **Speed moved; judgment didn't.** Industry numbers corroborate adoption without adding evidential weight (Adobe's 2024 Creative Trends survey: 83% of creative professionals integrating AI) — note your Chapter 4 reflexes firing: an adoption survey is a perception measure, not an outcome endpoint.

The misconception to correct in your own self-image: "designers are using AI to design." Mostly they are using AI to *draft and unblock*, with high-stakes work explicitly withheld — dense at the front of the workflow (analysis-as-summarization, development-as-drafting), sparse at strategy and evaluation. The workflow policy you will write this week largely formalizes what good practitioners already do by instinct; the point of writing it down is that instinct does not survive deadlines, reorgs, or new hires.

### Raises the floor, not the ceiling

Read the Moore et al. result precisely, because the precision is the finding. AI assistance reliably lifted **novice** output toward competence — *never-worse, sometimes-better* on a quality rubric, n=27, one course, one instructor team (single-source flag: the chapter's central positive finding rests on this one study; a companion ECTEL 2025 paper from the same team extends it to learner-side outcomes). It is **not** evidence that AI lifts expert output toward excellence, and **not** evidence about what happens to the novice — as opposed to the novice's artifacts — over time.

This **floor/ceiling distinction** is the chapter's organizing lens, and you have seen its signature before: Tutor CoPilot's gains concentrated among the lowest-rated tutors (Chapter 3) — AI compressing the bottom of a distribution toward the middle. The two ends have completely different economics. Floor-raising argues for AI as a quality-assurance layer on novice work. Ceiling-raising — currently unevidenced — would argue for AI as a substitute for expertise. Conflating them is how "our juniors produce better decks" becomes "we need fewer seniors," and that is a non sequitur: nothing in a floor-raising result implies the ceiling moved, and no comparable experiment on expert output exists. The ceiling half of this chapter's title claim is, at present, inference from absence — and the Evidence Box says so.

A useful classroom exercise makes the epistemics vivid: take the Moore data and write the two strongest *incompatible* conclusions it supports. "AI assistance raises novice output quality" and "novices learn less from assignments AI helps them complete" are both consistent with every rubric score in the study. The design that would distinguish them — longitudinal, with an unassisted *capability* endpoint for the designer — does not exist. You recognize this situation: it is Chapter 4's durability gap, relocated into your own career.

### Design fixation: the narrowing you don't feel

The best causal evidence on what AI assistance costs design thinking comes from HCI, not education. Wadinambiarachchi et al. (2024, CHI — between-participants experiment, N=60, visual ideation task): participants supported by an AI image generator showed **higher design fixation and lower fluency, variety, and originality** than an unassisted baseline. Two mechanism findings matter for LX work. First, fixation enters twice: *when writing the prompt* (the prompt commits you to a framing before exploration begins) and *when ideating in response to the output* (the generated artifact becomes the anchor). Second — the finding worth memorizing — **fixation displacement**: the designer's fixation shifts from the original exemplar onto the AI's outputs. The AI doesn't merely fail to diversify your ideation; it substitutes its own distribution for yours.

And the part that should genuinely worry you: participants experienced the AI as helpful while their measured creativity dropped — the designer-side echo of Act One's central trap, assisted performance up, capability signature down, with felt experience reporting only the first half.

For LXD this lands as the **design-by-analogy critique**: a model trained on existing course formats reproduces existing course formats. Ask for "a module on statistical inference" and you get the modal module — objectives, video, quiz — with high surface coherence and zero interrogation of whether that format serves these learners. The general critique (GenAI replicates training-data patterns rather than transforming design thinking) is associated with McCormack et al. 2024 [verify — SensiLab published closely matching critiques in 2023–2024, but the exact citation is unpinned]; the experimental ground is Wadinambiarachchi regardless of attribution. The misconception to surrender: "AI gives me more ideas, so my ideation is more divergent." The measured result is the opposite — more *output*, less *variety*. Volume of alternatives is not divergence if they are samples from one mode.

Name the compound risk while you are here: **polish-before-pedagogy.** Surface coherence now arrives before pedagogical assumptions have been tested, and a beautiful artifact is a more persuasive mistake. Fixation narrows what you consider; polish armors what you chose.

### The deskilling warning: signal, not verdict — and what to do about it anyway

Now hold the epistemic line exactly, because both overclaiming and dismissal are professionally costly. What exists: (a) adjacent-field documentation that creative practitioners report offloading, role-shift, and erosion-of-craft concerns as AI absorbs routine work (Palani & Ramos 2024 [verify — synthesis-sourced; venue unconfirmed]); (b) experimental evidence that AI assistance changes the cognitive character of design work in the moment — fixation, reduced divergence (Wadinambiarachchi 2024, verified); (c) Act One's mechanism evidence that offloading degrades unassisted capability in *learners* (Bastani 2025; the Kosmyna et al. cognitive-debt EEG result, carrying its single-study flag from Chapter 2). And the inferential step, marked in red: *designers practicing design are also learners*, so the crutch mechanism plausibly applies to them — but **no longitudinal study of designer capability under sustained AI assistance exists.** None. "What kind of designer does this workflow produce?" is open in exactly the way the durability gap is open for students.

So the deskilling claim is a **warning signal, not a causal claim** — and Chapter 4 taught you precisely what to do with that epistemic object. The risk is asymmetric: the cost of unnecessary unassisted practice is small; the cost of a cohort of prompt-fluent, judgment-poor designers is large, slow to detect, and hard to reverse. When you cannot measure the harm on your timescale, the burden of proof flips to the feature. That justifies structural protections for skill-building tasks *without* requiring the causal claim to be proven — which is fortunate, because it can't be, yet.

The LXD-specific sharpening: content generation, branching-scenario drafting, persona construction — the tasks GenAI absorbs most eagerly — are precisely the tasks through which junior designers historically *built* pedagogical intuition. They are the profession's practice problems. **Automating them is automating the apprenticeship.** The two symmetric misconceptions to refuse: "deskilling is proven — ban AI for juniors" (it is not proven; bans forfeit the floor-raising gains and push use underground — Mollick's research on workplace AI calls these hidden users "secret cyborgs," and policy that creates them audits nothing); and "no causal evidence — so the concern is hand-waving" (warning signals with mechanism evidence and asymmetric risk justify design responses before proof; that is what professional judgment under uncertainty *is*).

The positive frame is the **co-creative framing** from the 2025 *Journal of Applied Instructional Design* line: AI plus learning analytics as instruments the designer conducts — e.g., AI-assisted persona generation grounded in real learner data when direct research is scarce — explicitly *not* AI-as-author. The designer owns problem framing, pedagogical strategy, and evaluation; AI accelerates the material in between. Mollick's *Co-Intelligence* (2024) makes the strongest accessible case for inviting AI into everything; reconciling his enthusiasm with Wadinambiarachchi's data is not a compromise but a precision: Mollick's own "jagged frontier" — AI excellent and terrible at adjacent tasks with no visible boundary — *requires* exactly the task-level discrimination your workflow policy enforces.

### Narrow beats broad: tools, the NNG verdicts, and the policy that survives contact

The Nielsen Norman Group's multi-year review program is the field's only longitudinal record of AI design-tool value, and its trajectory is instructive. The 2024 assessment: few design-specific AI tools meaningfully enhanced workflows; practitioners mostly used general chatbots for drafting and brainstorming. The 2025 follow-up: "marginally better" — with one durable finding. **Specialized, narrow features genuinely save time** (rename layers, rewrite microcopy, summarize research notes), while broad "design this whole screen" generators disappoint — plausible-looking output that ignores flows, accessibility, content constraints, and edge cases (NN/g 2024; 2025). The designer's role shifts toward editor, curator, prompt-writer; judgment, prioritization, and systems thinking stay human. The procurement heuristic: **buy verbs, not magic** — evaluate any AI feature as a specific verb applied to a specific artifact, and distrust features whose claimed scope is "design." (Forecast caveat: that narrow tools are where value *permanently* lives is contestable — teach it as current evidence, not prophecy.)

Toolchain specifics, all carrying this book's extreme-aging flag — verify product names and capabilities before each offering. **Figma AI / Figma Make**: prompt-to-prototype compression of concept → mockup → clickable learner journey — genuinely useful for testing alternate flows, genuinely dangerous as a polish-before-pedagogy accelerant, because a clickable prototype of an untested pedagogical assumption is the most persuasive version of that mistake. **xAPI + LRS + AI analytics**: the quieter, more durable shift — real-time behavioral feedback loops at small-institution scale, formerly a major-platform privilege; the learning-analytics half of the co-creative framing and the data substrate Chapters 7–8 will assume.

Which brings everything to the chapter's deliverable. A workflow policy that survives contact with deadlines has three properties Act One already taught you: it is **structural, not aspirational** ("use AI thoughtfully" is a wish); it is **task-level** (a three-column table — AI drafts / AI assists with human review / never-touch); and it has an **enforcement surface** (a named thing a named reviewer checks). Its never-touch list needs two separate rationale columns, because students reliably build only the first: **risk-based** entries (learner data, grading, anything privacy- or stakes-bound) and **apprenticeship-based** entries — tasks done unassisted *because that is where the doer's own skill is built*. The gym analogy lands hardest with working professionals: you can hire someone to lift your weights; the weights move, you don't get stronger. Junior design tasks are reps, not output. The never-touch list is where designers are made.

## Pattern Card: The AI Workflow Policy (Never-Touch List)

**Pattern name:** AI Workflow Policy with a Two-Rationale Never-Touch List

**Trigger:** A designer or team adopts GenAI for any design task — or already has, ungoverned (the more common trigger).

**Structure:**
1. **Task-level table**, three columns: *AI drafts* (human edits and owns) / *AI assists, human reviews* (with the review criterion named) / *never-touch* (human-only).
2. **Two rationale tags** on every never-touch entry: *risk-based* (data, stakes, privacy) or *apprenticeship-based* (this task is where the doer's skill is built). An entry may carry both; every entry must carry one.
3. **Enforcement surface:** for each AI-assisted task, name what the reviewer checks *for* — specifically the pedagogical-assumption errors that polish conceals.
4. **Withdrawal answer:** one sentence stating what this team can still design well if the AI subscription lapses tomorrow, and which policy entries keep that true.

**Fading rule:** Apprenticeship-based entries fade *per person, not per team*: when an individual demonstrates unassisted competence on a task class (e.g., a quarter's storyboards reviewed clean), that task may move to "AI assists" *for them*. Risk-based entries fade only with changes to the underlying risk. (Note the move: fading on demonstrated competence is the pattern you will build for learners in Chapter 6.)

**Failure mode:** *Policy theater* — aspirational language with no task table and no enforcement surface; and its quieter twin, the *taboo-list collapse*: the never-touch column fills with ethically obvious entries while the apprenticeship entries — the ones that actually make designers — never get written, because no one wants to tell a busy junior to do something the slow way.

## Worked example: The DataWise 101 workflow decision memo

**Situation.** DataWise 101 is an introductory statistics online course — the Track A case — and its sponsoring program has approved adding an AI homework-help tutor (the integration you will spec in Chapter 6). The design team: Maya (senior LX designer, 11 years), Tom and Aisha (junior designers, 14 and 8 months), and a statistician SME with four hours a week. Leadership's mandate arrives with a deadline and a phrase Maya has learned to fear: *"AI-first content production."* Before anyone designs the learner-facing tutor, Maya has to decide how the team itself uses AI to build it — Design Lab #1's exact problem at production scale.

**The problem as the designer sees it.** Maya needs the floor-raising gains — the timeline is real, and Moore et al. gives her honest grounds to expect better junior drafts. But the tutor build runs through exactly the artifacts juniors learn from: learning objectives distilled from SME interviews, the misconception inventory for sampling distributions, hint text, item variants. Hand all of that to the model and she ships the tutor while quietly unbuilding her team.

**Process — including the dead ends.** Draft one is theater: "We use AI as a thought partner while keeping humans in the loop." Maya bins it inside an hour — no task table, no reviewer, no answer to *what may Tom paste into the prompt window at 6 p.m. on deadline day?* Draft two overcorrects: a near-ban for juniors, AI-drafting privileges for seniors only. Aisha names the two failure modes from this chapter's own evidence within a day: the ban forfeits never-worse-sometimes-better on the drafts the timeline needs, and it will push use underground — ungoverned, undisclosed, unauditable. (The chess-academy logic applies to her too, she notes, and at least she knows it.) The third dead end is subtler: the first task table tags every never-touch entry with risk rationales only. The misconception inventory ends up AI-drafted — it isn't sensitive data, after all. The SME catches the problem in review: the drafted inventory lists *generic* textbook misconceptions and misses the two errors his actual students make, because the inventory was never really a document — it was the apprenticeship task through which a designer learns how statistics students think. Polish concealed the gap; only the SME's four scarce hours caught it.

**Resolution.** The shipped memo is one page plus a table. **AI drafts:** item variants from human-written stems; alt text; summaries of SME interview transcripts; first-pass storyboard layouts; xAPI-grounded persona drafts (co-creative framing — grounded in real learner data, reviewed against it). **AI assists, human reviews:** hint-text drafts for the Chapter 6 ladder — review criterion named: *every hint checked against the misconception inventory, not for fluency* (fluency the model already has; alignment is what it fakes). **Never-touch:** learning objectives from raw SME interviews (apprenticeship); the misconception inventory (apprenticeship, plus the SME's scarce review hours); final hint-ladder sequencing (risk: the learner-facing guardrail); evaluation design (risk + apprenticeship). Each junior keeps one full unassisted build per quarter — the gym clause — and Maya's review explicitly checks AI-assisted work for pedagogical-assumption errors, not typos. The withdrawal answer: *if the subscription lapses, this team can still write objectives, inventories, and evaluation designs unaided, because the policy never let those skills externalize.*

**The lesson.** A workflow policy is not a values statement about AI — it is a task table with a reviewer, and its never-touch column is a bet about where designers come from.

**The limit.** The policy governs tasks, not incentives. Under a hard enough deadline, shortcut-seeking remains rational in the moment, and a policy Maya cannot staff (the SME's four hours nearly missed the inventory failure) is theater with a table. And the deepest limit is the chapter's own honest center: no longitudinal evidence says the gym clause works. It is a risk-asymmetry bet, not a proven protocol — and the memo's final line says so.

## What would change my mind

The chapter's conservative center — protect apprenticeship tasks despite unproven deskilling — would be substantially revised by a longitudinal study of designer capability under sustained AI assistance, across multiple teams, with an *unassisted* design-capability endpoint (not artifact ratings): novices working AI-assisted for two-plus years, then assessed on unaided problem framing, misconception anticipation, and evaluation design against matched unassisted-trained peers. If the assisted cohort matched or exceeded on unaided judgment, the apprenticeship rationale would collapse into the risk rationale alone, the gym clause would become optional, and this chapter's title would lose its second clause. Conversely, a finding that assisted-trained designers systematically miss pedagogical-assumption errors would convert the warning signal into a causal claim — and move several "AI drafts" entries into never-touch. The study that decides this does not yet exist; the first team to run it changes this chapter.

## Still puzzling

- **Does fixation transfer from visual to instructional ideation?** Wadinambiarachchi studied image-based ideation; the classroom demo suggests the effect travels to learning-activity design, but suggestion is not evidence. Plausible, untested.
- **Do co-creative methods improve outcomes or just speed?** AI-plus-analytics persona generation and journey-map drafting currently rest on design-case literature, not comparative studies. "Faster" is established; "better" is open.
- **Where exactly is the floor/ceiling boundary?** Does sustained assisted practice eventually raise ceilings (a skill-acquisition claim), or does never-worse-sometimes-better reflect a hard limit of pattern replication? No evidence either way — and the answer determines whether "AI as design partner" is a junior tool or a career-long one.
- **Can an apprenticeship survive its own automation anywhere?** Other fields (radiology, law, accounting) are running the same uncontrolled experiment on their juniors. Whose evidence will arrive first, and will it transfer?

## Key terms

- **Workflow compression** — AI collapsing the time between design stages, concentrated at ideation and drafting.
- **Floor-raising / ceiling-raising** — lifting the bottom of an output distribution toward competence vs. the top toward excellence; only the first is evidenced.
- **Design fixation** — premature commitment to an early framing or exemplar during ideation.
- **Fixation displacement** — fixation shifting onto the AI's outputs; its distribution substituting for the designer's.
- **Design-by-analogy critique** — GenAI reproducing familiar formats from training data rather than reasoning from learning needs.
- **Polish-before-pedagogy** — surface coherence arriving before pedagogical assumptions are tested.
- **Deskilling warning** — the evidence-backed, causally unproven concern that sustained AI assistance erodes the skills it substitutes for.
- **Never-touch list** — the policy column of tasks AI does not perform, justified as risk-based, apprenticeship-based, or both.
- **Co-creative framing** — AI plus learning analytics as instruments the designer conducts; not AI-as-author.
- **Secret cyborg** — Mollick's term for the employee using AI covertly under a restrictive policy; the failure mode of bans.

## LLM Exercise: The Policy Red-Team

Copy-paste the following into the LLM of your choice. Notice — again — the design: it refuses to run without your artifact, and it gates its critique behind your own predictions.

```
You are a red-team reviewer for AI workflow policies on learning design
teams. Follow these rules exactly.

RULE 1 — REQUIRED INPUT. I must paste my own draft AI workflow policy: the
task table (AI drafts / AI assists with review / never-touch), the rationale
tags, the enforcement surface, and the withdrawal answer. If I have not
pasted a draft, your ONLY permitted response is to ask for it. Do not
invent a sample policy. Do not proceed.

RULE 2 — I PREDICT FIRST. Before you critique anything, ask me to name, in
writing: (a) which single entry in my table I believe is most vulnerable to
"policy theater," and (b) which never-touch entry a deadline would breach
first, and why. Do not reveal your assessment until I commit.

RULE 3 — STRESS TESTS, ONE AT A TIME. Run three scenarios against my
policy, pausing after each for my revision:
  (1) DEADLINE: a deliverable is due in 18 hours and the never-touch task
      on the critical path is half done. What does my policy permit, and
      what will actually happen?
  (2) NEW HIRE: a junior joins mid-quarter, fluent with AI, never trained
      without it. Which apprenticeship entries apply, and who notices if
      they are skipped?
  (3) AUDIT: leadership asks for evidence the policy is followed. What can
      my enforcement surface actually produce?

RULE 4 — THE TWO-RATIONALE CHECK. If every never-touch entry is
risk-based, say so and require at least one apprenticeship-based entry,
asking first: "Which task taught YOU the most, and is it on this list?"

RULE 5 — VERDICT LAST. Only after my third revision, give your assessment:
strongest entry, weakest entry, the one sentence you would add — then the
strongest argument that my policy is too conservative. I need that side
too.

You may not write policy entries for me. You critique; I revise.
```

*Deliverable: the transcript plus your revised policy, with revisions tracked. The artifact your Design Lab #1 grade rests on is the revised policy and your reasoning — by your own new policy, this exercise is "AI assists, human reviews," and you are the human.*
