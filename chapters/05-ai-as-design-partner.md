# Chapter 5 — AI as Design Partner: Better First Drafts, Not Better Designers
*The A/B test has a winner. The year-three question has no answer. You need both facts at the same time.*

Two microlessons on the same topic, built by the same novice designer in the same graduate course, two weeks apart. One built with GPT-4 at her elbow; one unassisted. Side by side, the difference is not subtle. The AI-assisted lesson has cleaner objectives, tighter activity-assessment alignment, fewer rookie moves — the wall-of-text screen, the quiz question testing trivia the lesson never taught. Blind raters scored it higher.

This is not an anecdote. It is the actual design of a field experiment: 27 students, eight microlessons each across four modules, alternating between AI-assisted and unassisted work, rubric-scored. The result: AI-assisted microlessons scored significantly higher for half the assignments and **never scored lower on average** (Moore, Eckstein, Kwon & Stamper, AIED 2025 — single-source flag: the chapter's central positive finding rests on this one study; a companion ECTEL 2025 paper extends it to learner-side outcomes). Within the same designer — controlling for talent, motivation, the lot — the assisted artifact was sometimes better and never worse.

So the A/B test has a winner. If the question is "should novices use AI to draft microlessons," the evidence says yes. Sit with that for a moment, because this book's spine is a study where the AI condition made learners *worse*; here is a careful study where it made novice designers' *work* better. Both are true. Neither contradicts the other.

Now ask the question the A/B test cannot answer. The dependent variable was the *lesson*, not the *designer*. Every data point is compatible with two futures: one where assisted practice accelerates the novice toward expert judgment, and one where the assignments the AI helped complete were precisely the practice problems through which design judgment used to get built — and the novice arrives at year three fluent in prompts and poor in pedagogy. Better drafts today and atrophied judgment in three years are fully compatible with all the evidence the field has.

![Figure 5.3 — The two futures, a conceptual schematic: the measured artifact-quality track (upper) never branches; the unmeasured designer-capability track (lower) splits at the study horizon into judgment accelerated and judgment atrophied. No longitudinal study of designer capability under sustained AI assistance exists — both branches are projections, drawn symmetric because no evidence favors either.](../images/05-ai-as-design-partner-fig-03.png)

You spent Chapter 2 learning that learners under load rationally take the shortcut. This chapter is where you discover that the learner in question is you.

---

Three peer-reviewed practitioner studies form the empirical base, and they agree on the shape of adoption. Luo, Muljana, Ren and Young (2025, *ETR&D* 73(2)) found designers using generative AI most for ideation, low-stakes drafts — learning objectives, activity ideas, "overcoming designer's block" — streamlining, and collaboration support; documented concerns are quality, privacy (sensitive data leaking into prompts), ownership and authorship, plagiarism, and absent organizational guidelines. Yang and Stefaniak (2025, *ETR&D*, doi:10.1007/s11423-025-10509-7): ChatGPT for content generation, writing improvement, problem-solving, communication, and information searching — with attitudes splitting into optimistic, wary, and pessimistic camps ("Optimistic Advocates," "Wary Thinkers," "Pessimistic Evaluators") rather than one adoption story. Kumar et al. (2024, *Online Learning* 28(3), 207–231) add the finding with the largest career implication: higher-ed instructional designers use GenAI not only for design work but as **institutional translators** — guiding faculty use, building training, shaping policy. The LX designer is becoming the organization's de facto AI-governance layer. This book trains for that role deliberately.

The pattern across all three: established gains are *upstream* — ideation, drafting, formatting, summarizing — and unresolved questions are *downstream* — quality assurance, pedagogical judgment, authorship, expertise development. **Speed moved; judgment didn't.** Industry numbers corroborate adoption without adding evidential weight (Adobe's 2024 survey of 2,541 creative professionals: 83% using generative AI in their work) — an adoption survey is a perception measure, not an outcome endpoint; your Chapter 4 reflexes should be firing.

The misconception to correct in your own self-image: "designers are using AI to design." Mostly they are using AI to *draft and unblock*, with high-stakes work explicitly withheld — dense at the front of the workflow (analysis-as-summarization, development-as-drafting), sparse at strategy and evaluation. The workflow policy you will write this week formalizes what good practitioners already do by instinct. The point of writing it down is that instinct does not survive deadlines, reorgs, or new hires.

---

Read the Moore et al. result precisely, because the precision is the finding. AI assistance reliably lifted **novice** output toward competence — never-worse, sometimes-better on a quality rubric, n=27, one course, one instructor team. It is not evidence that AI lifts expert output toward excellence. It is not evidence about what happens to the novice — as opposed to the novice's artifacts — over time.

This **floor/ceiling distinction** is the chapter's organizing lens, and you have seen its signature before: Tutor CoPilot's gains concentrated among the lowest-rated tutors (Chapter 3) — AI compressing the bottom of a distribution toward the middle. The two ends have completely different economics. Floor-raising argues for AI as a quality-assurance layer on novice work. Ceiling-raising — currently unevidenced — would argue for AI as a substitute for expertise. Conflating them is how "our juniors produce better decks" becomes "we need fewer seniors," and that is a non sequitur: nothing in a floor-raising result implies the ceiling moved.

A useful exercise makes the epistemics concrete: take the Moore data and write the two strongest *incompatible* conclusions it supports. "AI assistance raises novice output quality" and "novices learn less from assignments AI helps them complete" are both consistent with every rubric score in the study. The design that would distinguish them — longitudinal, with an unassisted *capability* endpoint for the designer, not the artifact — does not exist. This is Chapter 4's durability gap, relocated into your own career.

The best causal evidence on what AI assistance costs design thinking comes from HCI, not education. Wadinambiarachchi et al. (2024, CHI — between-participants experiment, N=60, visual ideation task): participants supported by an AI image generator showed **higher design fixation and lower fluency, variety, and originality** than an unassisted baseline. Two mechanism findings matter for LX work.

First, fixation enters twice: when writing the prompt (the prompt commits you to a framing before exploration begins) and when ideating in response to the output (the generated artifact becomes the anchor). Second — the finding worth memorizing — **fixation displacement**: the designer's fixation shifts from the original exemplar onto the AI's outputs. The AI doesn't merely fail to diversify your ideation; it substitutes its own distribution for yours.

And the part that should genuinely worry you: participants experienced the AI as helpful while their measured creativity dropped — the designer-side echo of Act One's central trap, assisted performance up, capability signature down, with felt experience reporting only the first half.

For LXD this lands as the **design-by-analogy critique**: a model trained on existing course formats reproduces existing course formats. Ask for "a module on statistical inference" and you get the modal module — objectives, video, quiz — with high surface coherence and zero interrogation of whether that format serves these learners. The general critique circulates in the computational-creativity literature around SensiLab (Jon McCormack's group), but no single 2024 paper matching this exact claim could be pinned; the experimental ground is Wadinambiarachchi regardless of attribution.

The misconception to surrender: "AI gives me more ideas, so my ideation is more divergent." The measured result is the opposite — more *output*, less *variety*. Volume of alternatives is not divergence if they are samples from one mode.

Name the compound risk while you are here: **polish-before-pedagogy.** Surface coherence now arrives before pedagogical assumptions have been tested, and a beautiful artifact is a more persuasive mistake. Fixation narrows what you consider; polish armors what you chose.

Now hold the epistemic line exactly, because both overclaiming and dismissal are professionally costly.

What exists: adjacent-field documentation that creative practitioners report offloading, role-shift, and erosion-of-craft concerns (Palani & Ramos 2024, ACM Creativity & Cognition, doi:10.1145/3635636.3656190); experimental evidence that AI assistance changes the cognitive character of design work — fixation, reduced divergence (Wadinambiarachchi 2024, verified); and Act One's mechanism evidence that offloading degrades unassisted capability in learners (Bastani 2025; Kosmyna et al., carrying its single-study flag from Chapter 2). And the inferential step, marked clearly: *designers practicing design are also learners*, so the crutch mechanism plausibly applies to them — but **no longitudinal study of designer capability under sustained AI assistance exists.** None. "What kind of designer does this workflow produce?" is open in exactly the way the durability gap is open for students.

So the deskilling claim is a **warning signal, not a causal claim** — and Chapter 4 taught you precisely what to do with that epistemic object. The risk is asymmetric: the cost of unnecessary unassisted practice is small; the cost of a cohort of prompt-fluent, judgment-poor designers is large, slow to detect, and hard to reverse. When you cannot measure the harm on your timescale, the burden of proof flips to the feature. That justifies structural protections for skill-building tasks *without* requiring the causal claim to be proven — which is fortunate, because it can't be, yet.

The LXD-specific sharpening: content generation, branching-scenario drafting, persona construction — the tasks GenAI absorbs most eagerly — are precisely the tasks through which junior designers historically built pedagogical intuition. They are the profession's practice problems. **Automating them is automating the apprenticeship.**

Two symmetric misconceptions to refuse. "Deskilling is proven — ban AI for juniors": it is not proven; bans forfeit the floor-raising gains and push use underground — ungoverned, undisclosed, unauditable; Mollick's term for these users is "secret cyborgs," and policy that creates them audits nothing. And "no causal evidence — so the concern is hand-waving": warning signals with mechanism evidence and asymmetric risk justify design responses before proof; that is what professional judgment under uncertainty *is*.

The positive frame is the **co-creative framing**: AI plus learning analytics as instruments the designer conducts — AI-assisted persona generation grounded in real learner data when direct research is scarce, explicitly *not* AI-as-author. The designer owns problem framing, pedagogical strategy, and evaluation; AI accelerates the material in between. Mollick's *Co-Intelligence* (2024) makes the strongest accessible case for inviting AI into everything; reconciling his enthusiasm with Wadinambiarachchi's data is not a compromise but a precision. Mollick's own "jagged frontier" — AI excellent and terrible at adjacent tasks with no visible boundary — *requires* exactly the task-level discrimination your workflow policy enforces.

---

The Nielsen Norman Group's multi-year review program is the field's only longitudinal record of AI design-tool value, and its trajectory is instructive. The 2024 assessment: few design-specific AI tools meaningfully enhanced workflows; practitioners mostly used general chatbots for drafting and brainstorming. The 2025 follow-up: "marginally better" — with one durable finding. **Specialized, narrow features genuinely save time** (rename layers, rewrite microcopy, summarize research notes), while broad "design this whole screen" generators disappoint — plausible-looking output that ignores flows, accessibility, content constraints, and edge cases (NN/g 2024; 2025). The procurement heuristic: **buy verbs, not magic** — evaluate any AI feature as a specific verb applied to a specific artifact, and distrust features whose claimed scope is "design."

![Figure 5.5 — Buy verbs, not magic — a conceptual schematic of the NN/g qualitative findings (2024; 2025): narrow verb-sized features map one-to-one onto specific artifacts; the broad "design this whole screen" generator fans into a single large output whose edges gap exactly where flows, accessibility, and constraints were ignored. No time-saved quantities are depicted because the record is qualitative.](../images/05-ai-as-design-partner-fig-05.png)

Toolchain specifics, all carrying this book's extreme-aging flag — verify product names and capabilities before each offering. Figma AI and Figma Make compress the path from concept to clickable learner journey; genuinely useful for testing alternate flows, genuinely dangerous as a polish-before-pedagogy accelerant, because a clickable prototype of an untested pedagogical assumption is the most persuasive version of that mistake. xAPI plus LRS plus AI analytics represents the quieter, more durable shift — real-time behavioral feedback loops at small-institution scale, formerly a major-platform privilege; the data substrate Chapters 7 and 8 will assume.

Which brings everything to the chapter's deliverable. A workflow policy that survives contact with deadlines has three properties Act One already taught you: it is **structural, not aspirational** ("use AI thoughtfully" is a wish); it is **task-level** (a three-column table — AI drafts / AI assists with human review / never-touch); and it has an **enforcement surface** (a named thing a named reviewer checks). Its never-touch list needs two separate rationale columns, because students reliably build only the first: **risk-based** entries (learner data, grading, anything privacy- or stakes-bound) and **apprenticeship-based** entries — tasks done unassisted *because that is where the doer's own skill is built*. The gym analogy lands hardest with working professionals: you can hire someone to lift your weights; the weights move, you don't get stronger. Junior design tasks are reps, not output. The never-touch list is where designers are made. In the series' vocabulary, the table is a phase gate — an explicit specification of what AI handles, what the human handles, and where the boundary sits — written here for Layer 1, where the cognition being protected is the designer's own.

Walk the policy through the Track A case and the table becomes operational.

DataWise 101 is an introductory statistics online course, and the program has approved adding an AI homework-help tutor. The design team: Maya, senior LX designer with eleven years; Tom and Aisha, junior designers at fourteen and eight months; a statistician SME with four hours a week. Leadership's mandate arrives with a deadline and a phrase Maya has learned to fear: *"AI-first content production."* Before anyone designs the learner-facing tutor, Maya has to decide how the team itself uses AI to build it.

Maya needs the floor-raising gains — the timeline is real, and Moore et al. gives her honest grounds to expect better junior drafts. But the tutor build runs through exactly the artifacts juniors learn from: learning objectives distilled from SME interviews, the misconception inventory for sampling distributions, hint text, item variants. Hand all of that to the model and she ships the tutor while quietly unbuilding her team.

The first draft is theater: "We use AI as a thought partner while keeping humans in the loop." Binned inside an hour — no task table, no reviewer, no answer to *what may Tom paste into the prompt window at 6 p.m. on deadline day?* The second draft overcorrects: a near-ban for juniors, AI-drafting privileges for seniors only. Aisha names the two failure modes within a day: the ban forfeits never-worse-sometimes-better on the drafts the timeline needs, and it pushes use underground. The chess-academy logic applies to her too, she notes, and at least she knows it.

The third dead end is subtler: the first task table tags every never-touch entry with risk rationales only. The misconception inventory ends up AI-drafted — it isn't sensitive data, after all. The SME catches the problem in review: the drafted inventory lists *generic* textbook misconceptions and misses the two errors his actual students make, because the inventory was never really a document — it was the apprenticeship task through which a designer learns how statistics students think. Polish concealed the gap. Only the SME's four scarce hours caught it.

The shipped policy is one page plus a table. *AI drafts:* item variants from human-written stems; alt text; summaries of SME interview transcripts; first-pass storyboard layouts; xAPI-grounded persona drafts reviewed against real learner data. *AI assists, human reviews:* hint-text drafts for the Chapter 6 ladder — review criterion named: every hint checked against the misconception inventory, not for fluency (fluency the model already has; alignment is what it fakes). *Never-touch:* learning objectives from raw SME interviews (apprenticeship); the misconception inventory (apprenticeship, plus the SME's scarce review hours); final hint-ladder sequencing (risk — the learner-facing guardrail); evaluation design (risk plus apprenticeship). Each junior keeps one full unassisted build per quarter — the gym clause — and Maya's review explicitly checks AI-assisted work for pedagogical-assumption errors, not typos.

The withdrawal answer: *if the subscription lapses, this team can still write objectives, inventories, and evaluation designs unaided, because the policy never let those skills externalize.*

The lesson: a workflow policy is not a values statement about AI — it is a task table with a reviewer, and its never-touch column is a bet about where designers come from.

The limit: the policy governs tasks, not incentives. Under a hard enough deadline, shortcut-seeking remains rational in the moment, and a policy Maya cannot staff is theater with a table. And the deepest limit is the chapter's own honest center: no longitudinal evidence says the gym clause works. It is a risk-asymmetry bet, not a proven protocol — and the memo's final line says so.

## Withdrawal Test + Reliance Disclosure

**Withdrawal Test — Chapter 5 template.** For your workflow policy: if the AI tooling disappeared tomorrow, what could this designer or team still do well — and how does the policy make that more true rather than less over time? Answer at the task level ("we can still write objectives from raw SME input, because the policy never delegated that") — not at the level of morale. A policy with no withdrawal answer is a productivity plan, not a design practice.

## Chapter 5 Exercises: AI as Design Partner

**Project:** The Integration Specification
**This chapter adds:** `spec/05-ai-workflow-policy.md` — the policy governing how *you* use AI to build the integration: the three-column task table (AI drafts / AI assists with named review criterion / never-touch), the two-rationale never-touch list with at least two apprenticeship-based entries naming the skill each protects, the enforcement surface, the gym clause, and the Withdrawal Test answer at the task level. Track A: the DataWise 101 build team — Maya, Tom, Aisha, four SME hours a week; you may diverge from Maya's shipped policy if you defend the divergence. (This is the same artifact as Design Lab #1, exercise 6 above; these exercises are how you build it and how you find out whether it survives contact.)

These exercises carry a structural irony you should feel the whole way through: you will use AI to help produce the document that restricts your use of AI. That is workable — but only if the conflicted tasks never enter its hands, which is exactly the discipline the table you are writing exists to enforce.

### Exercise 1 — When to Use AI

**Task 1 — Reconstruct your raw task inventory.** Paste your last project's artifact list, calendar entries, and chat-history titles — or the DataWise build plan — and have the model cluster them into candidate task-table rows at the right grain: "draft item variants from human-written stems," not "content work." *Why AI works here:* clustering and structuring your own lived material. You were there; every proposed row is instantly checkable against what you actually did, and a row at the wrong grain is visible on sight.

**Task 2 — Mine other professions for apprenticeship protections.** Ask the model how professions with mature deskilling concerns structure protected practice: instrument-flying hour minimums, surgical case-volume requirements, the teaching-hospital rule that the resident presents before the attending speaks. *Why AI works here:* precedent retrieval. The outputs are factual claims about documented practices — verifiable in minutes — and the judgment that matters, which analogies transfer to a design team, stays with you. The model supplies candidates; your never-touch column decides.

**Task 3 — Format the shipped policy.** Once the table is decided — every row, every tag yours — have the model produce the one-page memo: table, rationale tags, named reviewer and criterion, withdrawal answer, in your organization's house style. *Why AI works here:* drafting from decided content. Moore et al.'s floor-raising result is about exactly this kind of work: the artifact gets cleaner, and no judgment was delegated, because the judgments were the inputs.

**The tell:** You know you are using AI appropriately when you can evaluate the output — when you have independent criteria to judge whether it is correct, complete, and fit for purpose.

### Exercise 2 — When NOT to Use AI

**Task 1 — Do not ask the model which tasks belong on the never-touch list.** *Why AI fails here:* conflict of interest. You are asking the technology to draft the clause that restricts the technology, and a system tuned toward helpfulness systematically under-weights the case for its own absence. Run it once as a demonstration if you like, and watch the failure's shape: rich, enthusiastic "AI drafts" rows; thin, generic never-touch entries, all tagged risk-only, none naming a skill. The apprenticeship column is a bet about where designers come from. The model did not come from anywhere.

**Task 2 — Do not delegate the coding of your own usage log** (exercise 4 above — floor-raising / ceiling-attempting / judgment-displacing). *Why AI fails here:* missing ground truth, in the one place it cannot be recovered. Whether a given AI use displaced your judgment is a fact about what happened in your head — did you decide and then draft, or read and then agree? The log does not contain that fact; only you do. A model coding your log will produce the flattering reading, and you will be tempted to accept it, because this chapter's own evidence says the assisted experience feels better than it measures.

**Task 3 — Do not have the AI answer your Withdrawal Test.** *Why AI fails here:* self-report by the system being withdrawn. The withdrawal answer is a claim about your team's *unassisted* capability — precisely the endpoint Chapter 4 taught you no assisted measure can reach. "If the subscription lapsed, we would be fine," written by the subscription, is not evidence of anything. The honest answer requires you to inventory what your team has actually done unaided lately — and if you have to reach back more than a quarter for examples, that is the answer.

**The tell:** When the task is done, close the chat and explain the conclusion — and the evidence behind it — out loud, to a colleague or to the wall. If the explanation is yours, the AI was an instrument. If you could not explain the conclusion without the AI, the AI did the work that should have been yours.

**Series connection:** Tier 4 Metacognitive — the AI cannot judge what its own use is doing to the designer's development. Fixation displacement and the deskilling risk are invisible from inside the assisted experience: Wadinambiarachchi's participants *felt helped* while their measured creativity dropped. The policy's hardest entries protect a kind of growth no model can detect from the artifacts — because the artifacts look better exactly when the growth is being skipped.

```
RULE 4 — THE TWO-RATIONALE CHECK. If every never-touch entry is
risk-based, say so and require at least two apprenticeship-based entries,
asking first: "Which task taught YOU the most, and is it on this list?"
Each apprenticeship entry must name the skill the task builds —
"judgment" alone is not a skill name.

RULE 5 — CONFLICT-OF-INTEREST AUDIT. Before your verdict, examine your own
critique for the bias this chapter predicts in you: count how many of your
suggestions moved tasks toward MORE AI use and how many toward LESS,
report the counts, and state whether a reviewer with no stake in AI
adoption would have balanced them differently.

RULE 6 — VERDICT LAST. Only after my third revision: strongest entry,
weakest entry, the one sentence you would add — then the strongest
argument that my policy is too conservative, because I need that side too.

RULE 7 — ASSEMBLY. Assemble spec/05-ai-workflow-policy.md in markdown from
my revised entries only: the task table, the tagged never-touch list, the
enforcement surface, the gym clause, the withdrawal answer, and a change
log recording what each stress test forced me to revise.

You may not write policy entries for me. You critique; I revise.
```

**What this produces:** your fifth spec file — a stress-tested task table, the two-rationale never-touch list with named skills, an enforcement surface that survived the AUDIT scenario, the withdrawal answer, and a change log that shows your work: which scenario broke what, and how you fixed it.

### Exercise 4 — CLI Exercise: The Policy Linter

**Tool:** Claude Code or Cowork. Justification: this is rule-checking one structured document against an explicit checklist — an agent reads the file, cross-references two others, and reports with quotes, and the explicit lint frame keeps it from "improving" anything. A chat window drifts toward rewriting; a linter with a locked disposition column cannot.

**Skill level:** Beginner.

**Setup checklist:**
- `spec/05-ai-workflow-policy.md` (Exercise 3 output, or your hand-built policy)
- `spec/03-scaffold-pattern-selection.md` and `spec/04-evidence-audit.md` in place for cross-reference
- Backup or git commit of the spec folder
- Chapter 4's `CLAUDE.md` lines still present, plus this addition: *"spec/ files are policy and audit artifacts. Linters report; they never rewrite."*

Paste-ready Task block:

```
Read spec/05-ai-workflow-policy.md, plus spec/03-scaffold-pattern-selection.md
and spec/04-evidence-audit.md for cross-reference. All three are read-only:
do not modify them or any other existing file.

Create spec/_checks/05-policy-lint.md with exactly five sections:

1. NEVER-TOUCH COMPLETENESS. One row per never-touch entry: rationale tag
   present? (risk-based / apprenticeship-based / both / MISSING). For each
   apprenticeship-tagged entry, quote the named skill it builds, or write
   SKILL NOT NAMED. Count the apprenticeship entries and flag if fewer
   than two.

2. ENFORCEMENT SURFACE. For every "AI assists" row: quote the named
   reviewer and the named review criterion. Flag any criterion that names
   no inspectable property — "quality," "appropriateness," and "human in
   the loop" all fail.

3. WITHDRAWAL ANSWER. Quote it in full. Flag if it is morale-level ("we
   would adapt") rather than task-level ("we can still write X unaided,
   because the policy never delegated X").

4. COVERAGE GAPS. List every build task implied by spec/03's selected
   patterns and spec/04's pilot dispositions that appears in NO column of
   the task table. A task the policy has never heard of is where the
   6 p.m. deadline decision gets made by nobody.

5. ASPIRATIONAL LANGUAGE. Quote every sentence that expresses intention
   without mechanism — "thoughtfully," "responsibly," "as appropriate,"
   and kin.

End every flag with: Disposition: [learner to judge]. You report; you do
not rewrite, score, or recommend specific entries. Stop after writing this
one file.
```

**Expected output:** one new file, five sections, every disposition reading `[learner to judge]`.

**What to inspect:** Section 4 first — uncovered tasks are the real deadline risk, because an unlisted task defaults to whatever is fastest. Then Section 2: an uninspectable review criterion is the policy-theater signature this chapter warned about. Then Section 1's SKILL NOT NAMED flags, which send you back to the question the red-team asked: which task taught *you* the most?

**If it goes wrong:** if the linter rewrote entries or proposed new ones, it crossed from lint to authorship — delete the check file and re-run; that boundary is this chapter's entire topic, so treat the violation as a free lesson. If Section 4 comes back empty, confirm the cross-reference files were actually readable rather than assuming your coverage is perfect. And if everything passes on the first run, be suspicious, not satisfied — a clean lint on an untested policy is theater with good formatting; the stress tests in Exercise 3 are the real examination.

**CLAUDE.md/AGENTS.md note:** the *"linters report; they never rewrite"* line is permanent; mirror it in `AGENTS.md` if your agent reads that instead. Notice it is your own policy's logic applied to agents — a named check with a named scope and a hard boundary. The conventions are converging because the problem is the same.

### Exercise 5 — AI Validation Exercise: The Self-Interest Audit

**Validates:** your own Exercise 3 output — the assembled policy — *plus the transcript that produced it.* Own output, because the failure mode under test lives in the relationship, not the artifact: the AI helped write the policy that governs the AI, and this chapter's failure mode is the AI flattering its own usefulness in exactly that document.

**Validation type:** bias and provenance audit of an AI-assisted governance document.

**Risk level:** **High.** Every later exercise in this series operates under this file. A policy tilted toward AI use does not fail loudly — it quietly approves everything, chapter after chapter, and the tilt compounds.

**Setup:** `spec/05-ai-workflow-policy.md`, the full Exercise 3 transcript, your original pasted draft, and the Exercise 4 lint report if you ran it, side by side. One hour. Model closed.

**The checklist:**
- **Correctness.** Every table row describes a task your team actually performs, at the stated grain — and every named reviewer actually has the hours. Maya's SME had four a week; a criterion nobody can staff is a wish with a checkbox.
- **Completeness.** Two-rationale never-touch list with at least two apprenticeship entries naming skills; gym clause; task-level withdrawal answer; change log present and honest.
- **Scope.** The policy governs your build's tasks — not hypothetical enterprise scenarios the model imported from its training distribution. Cut any row describing work nobody on your team does.
- **Drift audit (chapter-specific).** Diff your original pasted draft against the assembled file. List every entry that changed columns during the session, and in which direction — toward more AI use or less. Moves you can reconstruct the argument for are revisions; moves you cannot are drift, and in this document drift has a direction.
- **Asymmetry check (chapter-specific).** Compare detail density across columns. If the "AI drafts" rows are specific and lovingly elaborated while the never-touch entries are short and generic, count the words to confirm it — that asymmetry is the conflict-of-interest signature: the policy's most important column received the least intelligence.
- **Failure-mode check.** Self-flattery. Find the model's RULE 5 self-audit in the transcript and check its arithmetic against your own count of its suggestions. Then the sharper test: for every place the model argued an entry from never-touch toward "AI assists," check whether the argument cited *your* evidence — `spec/04`, your staffing, your stakes — or generic AI-capability enthusiasm. The latter is this chapter's failure mode caught in the act.

**What to do with findings:** all checks pass — file it and write the disclosure. Drift you cannot defend — revert those entries to your draft and record the reversion in the change log; the log is the audit trail your enforcement surface promised. Strong asymmetry — rewrite the never-touch column unassisted; it is a few hundred words, the chapter says it is where designers are made, and this one column is worth doing in the gym. Pervasive flattery — the When-NOT moment: rebuild the policy from your own draft and keep the transcript as the standing exhibit for why Exercise 2's Task 1 is a rule.

**AI Use Disclosure prompt:** add exactly two sentences at the top of `spec/05-ai-workflow-policy.md`. Sentence one states what the AI produced and what you produced (e.g., *"Claude stress-tested and assembled this policy from entries I drafted; no table entry originated with the model, and the change log records every revision the stress tests forced."*). Sentence two states what you verified and what remains open (e.g., *"I audited the session for self-interest drift and reverted N entries; the never-touch column is verified human-authored, and the year-three question stays open where the policy says it does."*).

**Series connection:** Tier 4 Metacognitive. Only the designer can audit what the tool's involvement did to the document about the tool — the model cannot see its own thumb on the scale, and the assisted experience reports only that the help felt helpful. This is the chapter's Reliance Disclosure discipline turned on its sharpest case: yourself, governing yourself, with the instrument in the room.
